The Finance Rules I Learned From Overpaying for a Watch

Practical finance tips from a watch collector who made the money mistakes first. Real numbers, honest trade-offs, and what actually builds wealth.

If you want your money to grow, the boring stuff wins: spend less than you earn, automate the saving, and keep your costs invisible. That's most of it. Back in 2019 I paid $3,200 for a pre-owned Omega Seamaster because a forum thread convinced me it was a steal, and I skipped funding my Roth IRA that year to do it. The watch is lovely. The $6,000 or so that money would be worth now if I'd invested it instead? That one still stings a little.

I've spent years around collectors who can quote the movement of a Lange 1 from memory but have no idea what their index fund charges them annually. It's the same skill, honestly. You're just valuing an asset, weighing what you pay against what you get, and being ruthless about the fees nobody talks about. So let me give you the tips that actually moved my net worth, not the ones that sound good on a podcast.

Pay Yourself First, Then Pretend the Money Was Never There

The single habit that changed my finances wasn't budgeting. I'm bad at budgeting. It was automation. On the day my income hits, a fixed amount moves out before I can touch it. Retirement account first, then a brokerage transfer, then a small sinking fund for the next watch so I stop dipping into savings for it.

Willpower is a terrible financial plan. The research backs this up hard. A landmark study on automatic 401(k) enrollment found participation jumped from around 37 percent to about 86 percent just by making saving the default instead of a choice you had to make. Read the actual paper if you like the details, it's Madrian and Shea's 401(k) research via NBER. Same brain, different default, wildly different outcome.

Start with whatever you can sustain. Even $50 a paycheck. The number matters less than never seeing it land in your checking account, where it turns into takeout and a strap you didn't need. I write more about this specific habit in my piece on finance tips that actually move the needle.

Fees Are the Termites of Your Portfolio

Here's the thing collectors understand that most investors don't: a 1 percent difference compounds into a fortune. We'll argue for an hour over a $200 servicing quote, then hand a financial advisor 1 percent of our entire portfolio every single year without blinking.

Run the math and it's brutal. On a $200,000 portfolio over 30 years, the difference between a fund charging 0.05 percent and an advisor plus fund charging 1.5 percent can erase six figures. The SEC's own investor bulletin on fees lays out how a 1 percent annual fee can cut your ending balance by roughly 28 percent over 20 years. That's not a rounding error. That's a house.

When I tested my own accounts against this a few years ago, I found I was paying a 0.68 percent expense ratio on a fund that did nothing a 0.03 percent index fund didn't do better. I switched. It took twenty minutes. In most cases, a low-cost, broadly diversified index fund beats the clever product your bank wants to sell you, and it does it while you sleep.

Build the Boring Buffer Before the Fun Money

An emergency fund is the least exciting thing in personal finance and the most important. Three to six months of real expenses, sitting in a high-yield savings account earning something, not in stocks, not in your watch collection, not anywhere you'd have to sell in a panic.

And here's where a lot of collectors go wrong. We treat the collection as an emergency fund. It isn't. A watch is an illiquid asset. When you actually need cash fast, you're selling into a buyer's market and eating a 20 to 40 percent haircut versus retail. I've watched friends unload a grail piece for far less than they paid because rent was due Friday.

  • Emergency fund: cash, liquid, boring, untouchable
  • Retirement: automated, low-fee, tax-advantaged first
  • The fun money: what's left, and only what's left

If your discretionary spending, watches included, is coming out of money that should be building your safety net, the order is wrong. Fix the order. Everything else gets easier. I break down more of this priority stack in my notes on building real wealth instead of the illusion of it. For more on this, see Finance Tips That Actually Work for Watch Collectors (And Everyone Else).

Debt, Discipline, and the Myth of the Appreciating Asset

Let me acknowledge the messy part, because most finance articles won't. Not all debt is evil, and not every splurge is a mistake. A low-interest mortgage while you invest the difference can be perfectly rational. High-interest credit card debt at 22 percent APR, though, is a fire you put out before anything else. There's no investment that reliably beats guaranteed 22 percent, so pay it first.

The misconception I want to kill is this idea that your watches are an investment. Some appreciate. A steel sports Rolex, sure. But the vast majority of watches, like the vast majority of cars, lose value the moment you walk out with them. Treat a purchase as consumption you enjoy, not a portfolio position, and you'll make cleaner decisions.

I'm not 100 percent sure this framing works for everyone, and there's a real debate among collectors about whether a few blue-chip references belong in a diversified net worth. Arguably they do, at a small allocation. Just don't let a hobby masquerade as a retirement plan. For the collector-specific angle, I wrote a whole thing on finance tips that work for watch collectors and everyone else.

Do one thing today: log into your brokerage or 401(k) and find the expense ratio on your biggest holding. If it's above 0.20 percent, look for the equivalent index fund and switch what you can this week. That single move, quiet as it is, will likely do more for your future than any watch you'll ever buy. And you can still buy the watch, once the boring stuff is handled.

Frequently asked questions

How much of my income should I actually be saving?

Aim for 15 to 20 percent of gross income toward retirement and long-term goals, but start with whatever you can automate without failing. Consistency beats the perfect percentage, and you can raise the amount every time you get a raise.

Is a luxury watch a good investment?

For a small handful of hard-to-get steel sports models, sometimes. For most watches, no. Treat a watch as something you enjoy owning, not as a retirement asset, because the vast majority lose value and are slow to sell when you need cash.

Should I pay off debt or invest first?

Pay off any high-interest debt above roughly 8 to 10 percent APR before investing, since no reliable investment beats that guaranteed return. Low-interest debt like a mortgage can run alongside investing, provided you have an emergency fund in place.
