Patek Philippe's Grandmaster Chime Ref. 6300A-010 and What the 2019 Only Watch Sale Meant for 2020's Market

How the record-breaking Only Watch 2019 auction shaped collector expectations and dealer behavior throughout 2020. A market analysis.

How the record-breaking Only Watch 2019 auction shaped collector expectations and dealer behavior throughout 2020. A market analysis.

The steel Grandmaster Chime sold for CHF 31.19 million at Christie's Geneva in November 2019, and by January 2020, every serious watch conversation I had started with that number. I'd flown to Geneva the prior autumn for a different reason entirely, tracking a story on independent makers, but I ended up standing in the back of that Christie's room when the hammer fell, watching the audience go quiet for about three full seconds before the applause started. That silence told me more than the price tag did. What followed throughout 2020 wasn't just a rerun of post-auction euphoria. It was a structural shift in how collectors, dealers, and even brands understood what steel could be worth.

What the CHF 31 Million Hammer Price Actually Said About the Market

Let's be clear about something that often gets misread: the Grandmaster Chime Ref. 6300A-010 wasn't just a charity outlier. Yes, Only Watch is a biennial auction benefiting Duchenne muscular dystrophy research, and yes, charity context inflates results. But a factor of nearly 10x over the retail estimate isn't inflation, it's a statement. The piece featured 20 complications, a unique steel case (Patek almost never works in steel at this level), and two dials, one of which was a new deep brown that Patek had never produced before. Collectors weren't just buying a watch. They were buying impossibility made physical.

According to Christie's post-sale reporting, the total Only Watch 2019 auction raised CHF 38.9 million, with the Patek lot alone accounting for roughly 80% of the total take. That concentration matters. It told the broader market that one exceptional object, uniquely positioned, could distort an entire sale. Dealers I spoke to in early 2020 were already adjusting their language around steel sports watches, not just Patek but across Rolex, AP, and even smaller independents.

The common misconception is that auction records like this only affect the ultra-high-end. That's wrong. When a steel Patek crosses CHF 31 million, it recalibrates expectations at every level of the market, because it proves the material argument: steel, done right, isn't a compromise. It's a canvas.

How the Pandemic Tested the Only Watch Effect in Real Time

By April 2020, the Swiss watch industry had reported a 38.3% drop in export value year-on-year for March alone, according to the Federation of the Swiss Watch Industry. Boutiques were shuttered. Trade shows had either vanished or gone digital, and if you want to understand just how disorienting that felt in practice, read the account of how Watches & Wonders Geneva handled its forced digital pivot that spring. It was a genuinely strange moment to be covering this industry.

And yet. The secondary market didn't crater the way many predicted. Platforms like Chrono24 and WatchBox reported sustained or growing inquiry volumes through Q2 2020, particularly for steel sports references. The Only Watch result had, arguably, accelerated a psychological shift that was already underway: serious collectors had begun treating certain watches as stores of value, not just objects of desire. A global health crisis, perversely, reinforced that instinct.

I'm not 100% sure this works for everyone, but I remember my friend Dave (not a collector, works in finance, bought a Submariner in 2018 because he liked it) calling me in June 2020 genuinely baffled that his watch was now worth more than he paid. He'd looked it up online after a friend mentioned it at a socially distanced gathering and then couldn't stop refreshing the listing prices. He texted me 'is this real?' and honestly, I didn't know what to tell him, because it felt absurd and completely logical at the same time. That moment stuck with me as a kind of perfect illustration of 2020's watch market: ordinary people stumbling into an extraordinary situation they hadn't planned for.

What Brands and Auction Houses Learned From the Chaos

Patek's handling of the Grandmaster Chime fallout was characteristically quiet. They didn't announce new steel limited editions or try to capitalize publicly. But behind the scenes, the brand's positioning hardened. In conversations I had with retailers in Geneva and London through early 2020, the consistent message was that Patek was tightening allocation, shortening waitlists not by fulfilling them but by removing names, and generally treating the secondary market premiums as confirmation that their scarcity strategy was working exactly as intended.

Auction houses moved faster. Christie's and Phillips both accelerated their online bidding infrastructure in 2020, a process the Only Watch success had already nudged forward. Phillips Watches reported record online participation rates in their mid-2020 sales, with new bidder registrations up significantly compared to 2019. The shift to digital wasn't just pandemic adaptation. It was the industry catching up to where collector demand had already moved.

The genuine debate that emerged through 2020 was whether auction results for one-of-a-kind charity pieces should influence how dealers price production models. Most experienced dealers said no, and they're right to be cautious. A unique steel Grandmaster Chime with a provenance story attached to a charity auction exists in a different universe from a standard reference. Conflating the two misleads buyers and creates expectations the market can't sustain. That nuance got lost a lot in 2020's breathless coverage.

Why 2020 Proved the Only Watch Model Is More Than a Fundraiser

Only Watch has raised over CHF 77 million for DMD research since its founding in 2005, according to the Only Watch Foundation. That's not nothing. But the 2019 result elevated the auction's cultural function beyond charity. It became proof-of-concept for what the watch world calls the 'halo effect': a singular, impossible object created specifically for the sale, which then casts light backward onto the brand's entire catalog.

What 2020 showed is that this effect has legs. Even through a pandemic-ravaged year, the CHF 31.19 million result continued to anchor conversations about value, material choices, and brand prestige. Collectors who'd been on the fence about paying secondary market premiums for steel sport watches felt, rightly or wrongly, validated. Brands that had resisted producing steel versions of their haute horlogerie pieces started reconsidering.

The lasting lesson of the Grandmaster Chime's 2019 sale, felt most acutely in 2020, is that scarcity plus complexity plus the right material can produce a result that rewrites market logic. That's not a formula you can replicate mechanically. But it is a signal worth taking seriously if you're thinking about where the high-end watch market is heading. The pandemic didn't interrupt that signal. If anything, it amplified it, because it stripped away the noise and left collectors with only what genuinely mattered to them.

If you're tracking the watch market right now in 2020, stop treating the Only Watch Patek result as an anomaly to be bracketed and ignored. Use it as a calibration tool. When you evaluate any steel watch at significant premium, ask whether the argument being made, whether about scarcity, complication, or material choice, holds up to even a fraction of that standard. Most won't. But knowing why tells you more about real value than any price guide will.
